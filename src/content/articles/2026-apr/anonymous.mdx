## Anonymous Article

This year, the National Basketball Association’s unanimous Defensive Player of the Year was Victor Wembenyama—a Frenchman with a towering height of 7 '5 and an 8’ wingspan greater than that of a bald eagle. Obviously, NBA players are freakishly tall; the average height of an NBA player is 6 '7, yet Victor Wembanyama stands a head taller than everybody else. With his colossal stature and his French background, one would think the Eiffel Tower is a fitting nickname. But the NBA doesn’t do repeats, there is only one Answer, there is only one Joker, and only one Black Mamba. And similarly, there is only one Stifle Tower; his name is Rudy Gobert. 

Rudy Gobert, like Wembenyama, is a 7-foot Frenchman known for his defensive prowess. Gobert was drafted 27th overall in the 2013 draft. It is safe to say that coming into the league, the expectations for him were certainly low. Beginning his career with the Jazz, he had a slow start to his career, averaging only 2.3 points a game on 9.6 minutes. Yet, something in his stats stood out. At 9.6 minutes per game, Rudy Gobert averaged nearly a block per game. Something only a few really achieve. Keep in mind Cooper Flagg, the 2025-26 Rookie of the Year, often compared to LeBron Raymone James Sr’s rookie season (one of the greatest rookie seasons in NBA history), only averages one block over the span of 30 minutes per game. Rudy Gobert’s defensive ability shone—a diamond in the rough. 

Obviously, his talent did not go unnoticed; in just five years, he had won his first Defensive Player of the Year 

award. His achievements did not stop there. In just the span of 2018-2022, Rudy Gobert won the award 3 more times. 
By the time Rudy Gobert had secured his fourth Defensive Player of the Year award, the conversation around him had already begun to shift. No longer was he just a late first-round pick who exceeded expectations—he had become the standard. Anchoring elite defenses year after year, altering shots that never even show up on the stat sheet, Gobert redefined what dominance looks like in a league obsessed with scoring. While players like Victor Wembanyama may capture attention with unprecedented physical tools and highlight-reel plays, Gobert’s greatness is rooted in consistency and discipline. And that’s the point: in a sport that glorifies offense and flash, Rudy Gobert proves that impact isn’t always loud. Sometimes, the most valuable presence on the court is the one opponents are too afraid to challenge at all.
