## Neurodiversity Awareness and Acceptance Month

By Danika Pierce (10)

April is Neurodiversity awareness month, which aims for the inclusion of neurodivergent individuals. According to the APA Dictionary Psychology, the definition of neurodivergent is “adj., n. neurocognitive functioning that differs from the neurological majority. It commonly includes conditions such as autism spectrum disorder, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder, dyslexia, and dyspraxia, but is not limited to formal diagnoses and lacks a single universally accepted classification. It may also be applied to individuals with multiple differences, known as multiply neurodivergent. [coined by Kassiane Asasumasu, a multiply-neurodivergent neurodiversity activist].” The most recent movements on mental health have been for not only awareness but acceptance.

The majority of people have developed awareness. However, all of this knowledge has been reduced to individuals coining phrases like, “My attention span is so short. I keep on scrolling! I must have ADHD.” In this case, knowing a neurodivergent condition, ADHD, has been reduced to humor. Awareness of neurodiversity is joked about in common practice. Other popular phrases are “You’re so stupid. You’re autistic!” and “Oh, I’m so OCD!” These individuals that express this humor rarely have any intention of getting a formal diagnosis or looking into the topics critically.

Why have empathy? Allow me to tell you a story. My little brother, an elementary school child, believes that he could never go to college. It is difficult to comprehend, as he is far too young to think so negatively of future prospects. Additionally, he has a curiosity that any teacher in the right mind would appreciate. He enjoys both science and fiction. His teachers have been impressed by his high vocabulary and emotional intelligence. What has convinced him that he cannot be taught? My little brother is of the group that society has labelled as “deficient”, as he has both dyslexia and ADHD. His high vocabulary came from deciphering conversations instead of from reading. His reading teacher looked down on him for struggling with reading, and many of his teachers, irritated with his different needs, refused to approach his learning in a different way than others, which led him to almost be labeled as a behavioral issue. He has been on the earth long enough to hear all the popular jokes. There are plenty of other children like him that have been taught that they are stupid because they are different. Why do we choose to put down children for something they cannot change?

Reversing the mistake of teaching people that thinking differently is lacking is possible. We must simply accept that not everyone’s brain is the same and show a little kindness in our word choice. Our school presented a nice example with the bubbles of kindness event. Students wrote notes on how to be kind to others. Afterwards, they ate cookies and blew bubbles. The enjoyable activity put less emphasis on pointing out differences between people and more on how to be positive towards all people. This encompasses the core idea of acceptance: treat people, who think differently, as human instead of as “deficient”.
