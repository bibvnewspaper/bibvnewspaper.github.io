## Why is the divorce rate so high?

Why is the divorce rate so high? I’ve heard this question numerous times. First, a clarification is called for: the divorce rates have been lowest in decades, based upon Pew Research. Still, people often consider the divorce rate to be too high, so an explanation shall be given on why people divorce at all, the historical importance of divorce to the patriarchy, and who “has it worse”. 

Divorce and the patriarchy are highly correlated. Originally, divorce had been highly stigmatized in the United States and considered social death. Women in particular would lose their economic and social position after divorcing. Additionally, getting a divorce was a challenge, as the partner’s offense had to be proven. The process had been costly and ultimately favored men. This ended with the no-fault divorce laws, which had been adopted by all states by 2010. These laws meant that fault no longer had to be proven. Organizations, such as NAWSA, National American Women Suffrage Association, advocated for this change. Poverty is frequent in the women’s divorce experience, as women are twice as likely to become poor after divorce compared with men. A cultural example of this can be given by the song “He Thinks He’ll Keep Her” by Mary Chapin Carpenter. The song describes the struggles a woman of the 1970s has in the caretaker position of her marriage, and the poverty of entering the “typing pool at minimum wage”. There were few career positions for women at the time, so many of these divorced women worked in the typing pool, which was an office that handled documents. The patriarchy has affected divorced men as well. Women are often pushed into the caretaker role. For this rea	son, men have been concerned over their ability to gain custody over their children or gain a beneficial agreement. This led to the creation of the Father’s Rights Movement. 

After providing the historical significance, the reasoning behind divorcing can be found. A study from a Couple Family Psychol article, which can be found in the National Library of Medicine provides statistics: 75.0% for lack of commitment, 57.7% for too much arguing, 45.1% for marrying too young, 36.7% for financial problems, and 23.5% for violence. This confirms that people divorce for a reason. 

Who has been spited by this system? Men? Women? Children. To clarify, this is not a “Parents should stay together for the kids” argument. To put it plainly, that is silly, as unhappiness is a toxin to a family. In divorce cases, courts often push for 50/50. That has been a popular effort by state governments to be a “50/50 state”. Washington is an example. This is under the argument that divorce courts are doing the best for the child, but 50/50 harms the child. In parenting, the two individuals are rarely equals.The court is not designed for the said child to articulate that. For instance, in Washington, a guardian ad litem, GAL, represents the child in court rather than the child directly testifying. This is done to protect the youth from potential repercussions. However, the average price of a GAL is $1,500 to $3,500. Individuals, especially women, tend to lack money post-divorce, so such expenses are unrealistic for one voice. Additionally, mental assessments of the parents are not required and are only asked for by the court in extreme cases. Being provided this information could significantly increase a judge’s ability to determine which parent is best fit to be a parent at all. 

Children have no power in divorce cases. There is an inclination to villainize divorce in this society, but such words are unhelpful. Divorce occurs for a reason, as history and statistics have confirmed. As a community, it is out of our capacity to change the structure of the divorce court. Nevertheless, we can recognize that there are children, particularly the younger ones in our school community, that require their parent’s support for their adolescent development but are in the instability of the divorced household. A child only actually requires the support of one stable adult, and that person does not need to be a parent. Community support can change a life.
