## An Open Letter to Being a Girl

By Dr. Thies

“But, you’re a girl”
An open letter to our female students:
To our young women,
There are two things that I want you to know.  The first is that you are capable of anything, and the second is that you are the only one that gets to define who you are.  Throughout my life, I have heard the phrase, “But you’re a girl.”  This was especially true when I fell in love with sports, decided to join the Army, and become a Police Officer.  While many people felt like they had to point this out as some type of character flaw that would prohibit me from excelling, I chose to ignore their preconceived notions and think of it more as a superpower.  “Yes, I’m a girl!”
I have never been ashamed to be a girl, and I am proud of who I am as a person overall.  I love the fact that I may not fit someone’s description of what they believe I should be.  That just makes me beautifully unique.  As a child I was athletic and also smart.  I loved to play in the dirt, play touch football with the neighborhood boys, ride bikes, and play soccer.  I absolutely hated dresses and flat out refused to wear them. 
I eventually grew out of this in my teens and did not discover my absolute love of a fabulous heel until well into adulthood.  I despised my hair.   During adolescence, it was nothing short of an inconvenience.  It was not pretty and straight like all my friends, and it always seemed to be in the way.  It was only in my adult years that I embraced the fact that my hair was a feature that I was proud to have as part of my overall identity.  It makes me smile when I hear people say things like, “You know, the one with the long, crazy curly hair.”
Who you are and what you like should be an evolving process.   Embrace the journey of getting to know yourself.  Throughout life, you will discover new facets of yourself and let go of old ones.  You are a work in progress.  The most important thing to remember as you learn and grow is that you are amazing and you hold worth.  You define what it means to be a girl and it is an honor to carry that identity.   
Imagine where we would be if people like Harriet Tubman, Marie Curie, Amelia Earhart, Rosa Parks, Anne Frank, Frida Kahlo, Aretha Franklin, Ruth Bader Ginsberg, Malala Yousafzai, Mia Hamm and the Williams sisters had simply stopped pursuing their passions when they heard the phrase, “But, you’re a girl.”  You, as young women, hold power alongside an abundance of potential.  This month we take the time to appreciate all of the obstacles that women have overcome.  I am here to tell you; we are not finished.  There is so much more to do and soon it will be your turn.
My generation of women and the women that came before me can only do so much.  Collectively, we have worked hard to break glass ceilings, disprove theories, win championships, break world records, and change laws.  We have all done this work for ourselves but also for all of you.  So, as we wrap up Women’s History Month, I want to let you know that you can now be a part of changing the narrative.
You are so ready and so capable of creating new change.  It is time to stop hearing, “But you’re a girl,” and start exclaiming, “Yes, we are girls and to be honest, the world better watch out!"  With all of you as a part of the next generation of thinkers, innovators and creators there is no limit to what women can accomplish.  
Happy Women’s History Month
Go Mountaineers,
Dr. Thies
