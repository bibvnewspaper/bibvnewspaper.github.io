## Views on Project Hail Mary

Written by Suri Song, 8Pt.

With a record breaking box office opening on March 20, *Project Hail Mary* is flourishing with high praise from movie enthusiasts and avid movie goers alike. Adapted from the science fiction novel by Andy Weir, PHM currently has a $96%$ on Rotten Tomatoes, a $4.4$ star rating on Letterboxd, and generated an estimate of $ $80.6$ million its opening weekend domestically, rivaling movies like ‘Oppenheimer’. Attesting to the movie’s popularity on a personal account, I went to see it opening night at 11PM in a virtually filled out theater. (The 7PM show was completely full). The movie ended at 2AM with a mountain of people exiting.

Project Hail Mary is an ambitious and emotionally resonant film that reimagines blockbusters and sci-fi cinema. As someone who recently read the book and holds it in extremely high regard, I approached this adaptation with exceedingly high expectations.

The movie succeeds in delivering a compelling and deeply human story (ironic) that isn’t afraid to pull on its audience’s hearts. It balances humor, tension, and emotional weight remarkably well, and provides seamless transitions from light to darker scenes. Ryan Gosling gives a standout performance as Ryland Grace, not only capturing both the character’s wit, but also his vulnerability and fear. The film never shies away from fear, grounding its premise in something profoundly human. Sandra Huller as Eva Stratt is equally impressive, with one prominent scene that moved me greatly. 

Visually, this film is everything. The cinematography and visual effects are exceptional (I wouldn’t expect anything less from Greig Fraser), particularly in its vivid depictions of space. I will say that the scene in the climax of the movie might be one of the most exhilarating and insane sequences I have ever seen in my life. The set is amazing… Did you know they didn’t use a single green screen in the whole movie? Any scene inside the Hail Mary was a set. Furthermore, Rocky was a genuine puppet controlled by his voice, James Ortiz. 

The score further elevated the experience, I can’t even describe it in words.

That said, some of the novel’s scientific depth (personally an absolute sucker for hard sci-fi) and character complexity are inevitably lost in translation. Certain plot elements and thematic nuances are streamlined or omitted, which undeniably made me vaguely disappointed. While this is understandable for a cinematic format, it does lessen some of what made the book so compelling for me personally. I have similar sentiments for other sci-fi adaptations like Dune. I also don’t like how the original novel ended, paired with the very rushed ending of the adaptation, I was mildly critical in that regard. The book is also incredibly well paced, but a lot of scenes in the movie felt rushed. 

Speaking for a more general audience, I’ve heard people say that they don’t like Andy Weir’s writing style, stating that it feels ‘cringe’, ‘unrealistic’, or ‘cliche’. I can see that. I think the movie’s script would appeal more to people who have these sentiments towards the novel, yet some of the original tone still remains. I don’t have a problem with the dialogue myself, as one, it’s a Reddit, software developer type humor that I am well accustomed to, and two, I think the overall story and the scientific detail is much more complex and interesting to focus on the dialogue… there’s bigger fish to fry, if you don’t like dialogue that doesn’t feel entirely human, don’t read books/watch movies written by software developers.

Despite these shortcomings, the movie preserves the core of the story, which is the relationship between Grace and Rocky. It remains a strong and faithful adaptation overall. I saw someone on Letterboxd say that they agree with every criticism given to this movie, but still love it. That’s how I feel.

Overall opinion? I loved the movie, and I definitely recommend everyone to go see it. Please go see it in IMAX. The movie was filmed on 70mm IMAX film, and watching it in the IMAX aspect ratio gives not only higher resolution for the insane visuals, but also gives significantly more detail, filling your peripheral vision. Watching it on a normal theatre screen will sometimes crop out parts of the picture, not recommended for a highly visually strong movie like PHM. I can go on longer and more arduous tangents on why you should watch PHM in IMAX in person, so either find me to learn more, or take my advice and go watch it in IMAX. Thanks for coming to my Ted Talk, go watch Project Hail Mary in IMAX.
