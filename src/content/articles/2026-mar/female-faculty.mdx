## Why is Women’s History Month important to you?

Ms. Iliescu: “For so long, women’s contributions to society went unacknowledged. Today, we have the opportunity to change that: we learn about and recognize them and express our gratitude. It also reminds me of my childhood and of all the women who influenced my life. …”

Ms. Delp: “History has always been an interest of mine. In school, I almost double majored in Biochemistry and History. … I have enjoyed learning stories about Susan B. Anthony, Madam Curie, Sally Ride and Jane Goodall. Hearing their stories and seeing the challenges that they faced in their careers are an inspiration to me.”


## Who is a woman in history that inspires you, and why?

Ms. Iliescu: “Queen Marie of Romania. She played a crucial role in shaping modern Romania. What inspires me most is her dedication: she knew nothing about the country before marrying into the royal family and moving there, but she ultimately fought for it more passionately than many politicians. ((Fun fact: In Washington state, there is a museum dedicated to her, the Maryhill Museum of Art.)”

Ms. Delp:  “Sally Ride has always been someone that inspired me as a child and in my life. … Later, when I was in my 20s, I had the chance to meet Sally Ride in person … I was a fly on the wall listening to these women … talk about the workplace. … I hadn’t realized that the workforce was so different for my mother’s generation. …”
 
## What do you hope students learn from Women’s History Month?

Ms. Iliescu: “I hope they learn that women have contributed to and continue to contribute to our society in diverse ways. It is not about being famous, but about showing compassion, kindness, resilience, creativity, curiosity, and courage.”

Ms. Delp: “I hope students hear these stories and realize that these issues are still facing women in society and the workplace. Sexual harassment, workplace discrimination and inequities in pay need our constant attention. … I think these type of celebrations are important so that people learn about these issues … during celebrations where a more diverse group of individuals may participate.”

## What is the coolest thing you’ve ever done?

Ms. Iliescu: “I learned to swim at 29, by myself. Before moving to the US, I lived in Cyprus for a few years, by the sea (the Mediterranean Sea.) One day I decided to swim to some big rocks that were sticking out of the water. It was hard because of the waves and wind, but when I got there, it was so cool to see the shore in the distance; it was an awesome sight. And then, I had to swim back.”

Ms. Delp: “During the premiere of the 2009 sequel to the 2007 documentary Two Million Minutes, Michael and Olga Block asked me to join them at the premiere in Washington D.C. At the event, I met Al Sharpton and Newt Gingrich, people I had studied in my high school government class. After the show, we went to Georgetown to eat dinner. When I sat down, I noticed Robert Redford was at the table next to me. The entire experience felt surreal. The movie validated the work we were doing. I had joined Teach for America
to help make education better for all children, but I felt the work that I was doing at
BASIS was actually helping to accomplish that goal.”


## What are some obstacles that you have faced because you are a woman?

Ms. Iliescu: “My dad was an officer in the army, and when I was in middle school, I wanted to follow the same career, to apply to a military high school. At that time, very few girls, almost none, were in the program. All the adults around me were against it: it is not appropriate for a woman to be in the army, and I wasn’t allowed to do it. Sometimes I wonder what my life would be like if I could do it?”

Ms. Delp: “After having my second child … I was asked by the new HR Director to relinquish my title so that they could hire someone else in the position. My baby was two days old, I was on maternity leave … In hindsight, I don’t think it was fair that I was asked to give up my position. … As more women started to have babies in the company, I became a mentor. …”

## Famous Female Mathematicians or Inspiration

> Are there female scientists whom you think deserve more recognition from students?

*Mary Everest Boole*! She was a mathematician and a collaborator of her husband, George Boole; she helped him with his work, but she never received recognition for it. She is referred to as George Boole’s wife. And she is not the only one who worked with and supported their husband, father, or brother with their academic work. There are many other women, hidden figures, who deserve recognition.

How do you think Women’s History Month can encourage more girls to feel confident in math and STEM subjects?

By learning about women who succeed in these subjects against all the obstacles, the girls could get confident that they can do it too. Nowadays it is easier for a girl to follow this path, and there are so many satisfactions from this field. 

How have your experiences as a woman influenced your career? For example, I acknowledge the fact that you lived in Romania for a long time and was a Bellevue School District teacher for many years.

I don’t know if being a woman influenced my career, but I know for sure that being a mom did. I have always seen my students the way I see my own children as students: how do I want the teacher to treat them? Then, I make sure to treat my students the same way.

How have your experiences as a woman influenced your career? For  example, I
know you have worked in the BASIS network for a long time and you know the founder
of BASIS. (PERSONALIZED TO DELP)

“I have had the pleasure of working for two female visionaries in my career, Wendy Kopp with Teach for America and Olga Block who founded BASIS Schools in 1998. … Her never give up attitude was something that I connected with … After Teach for America, I joined BASIS Scottsdale during its founding year, 2003. … Olga never accepted that something couldn’t be done. When she selected me to run a school at the age of 28, I had people that thought I was too young to be a principal. Olga always believed in me and continued to help me advance my career …”

Why is it important for younger students to learn about inspiring women early in
school? 

I think it is important for younger students to hear about women in history that made
pivotal changes in society. It sparks interesting debates and help children learn how to
form an opinion. They also learn that change is part of life and that society is constantly
changing.
